Good Leaders Prioritize. GREAT Leaders Posteriorize (v1)

MDE Leadership Blog Post
August 4, 2025 · 2 min · 311 words · Antonio Martinez

This blog post is part of the MDE program performance challenge

From Priorities to Posteriorities#

Prioritization is a fundamental leadership skill that every good leader masters. It’s relatively straightforward—we analyze tasks, assess their impact, and rank them by importance. Most leaders excel at identifying what matters most and organizing their teams around these priorities. But here’s the challenge: we often end up with too many “priorities,” stretching our limited bandwidth thin.

This is where I’ve personally struggled. Prioritization has always been my Achilles’ heel. I naturally gravitate toward helping everyone and tend to prioritize tasks I enjoy—especially those involving interesting challenges or opportunities to support others. While this feels rewarding, it often derails me from achieving higher-impact objectives.

The Power of Posteriorities#

The difference between good and great leaders lies in mastering posteriorities—the deliberate art of deciding what not to do. While prioritizing is about choosing what’s important, posteriorizing requires you to have the courage to actively set things aside, even when they seem valuable.

Defining posteriorities is inherently difficult because someone, somewhere, will always consider your posteriority their top priority. As leaders, our responsibility is to empathize with these perspectives, understand what motivates different stakeholders, and communicate our reasoning clearly. Sometimes we can offer workarounds; other times, we must firmly explain our decision and invite those who disagree to make a compelling case for reprioritization.

The key is being empathetic yet decisive. If our reasoning is sound, we shouldn’t fear moving items to the posteriority list. When we do shift existing priorities, we must immediately revisit our goals, adjust timelines, and reset expectations. Otherwise, we risk overcommitting and creating frustration—both for ourselves and those who depend on us.

Learning to say “no” and embracing posteriorities has helped me balance what I enjoy with what truly drives impact. Great leaders don’t just choose what to do, they courageously choose what not.
